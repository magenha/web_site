Our genealogy and genetic research

Thank you for expressing your interest. Here, we present an overview of our ongoing endeavors. While the majority of our efforts are concentrated in Cuban branches, we're also actively engaged in tracing genealogies in Nicaragua and Panama.

Our work encompasses both genealogical research and DNA testing, aimed at unraveling the historical carriers of specific haplogroups over the centuries.

If you're passionate about advancing research in a particular lineage or wish to contribute to our cause, we invite you to consider making a donation and reaching out to us . Your support can help us expedite our research and achieve meaningful results

0c4713

 E-M2

This line represents the descendants of a lineage of men from West Africa. They lived in the towns of San Jose de las Lajas, Tapaste and Santa Maria del Rosario. It is still being investigated, but its origins must be linked to the sugar mills in that area, where the Portugalete Mill (San Jose de las Lajas) was very important. See more on our Blog.

Lorenzo Gonzalez was a man ho lived during the second half of 19th century in the area between Santa Maria del Rosario and Tapaste. He had at least an illegitimate child with Candelaria Pagan, a lady who lived in Tapaste, of african ancestors. Actual research its being carried to obtain more information about Lorenzo and his ancestors.

cc5619

 E-V22

This lineage signifies the legacy of the Alcala surname in Havana and San Jose de las Lajas, Cuba. Individuals with this haplogroup often trace their roots to Arabic countries, evident from the phonetics of the Alcala surname. 'Alcala' is of Arabic origin, translating to 'from the Castle' in Spanish, which could be indicative of ancient Arab genetic origins in Spain. This aligns with the historical Arab presence and influence during the period of occupation in Spain.

Juan Alcala Guerra, son of Jose Alcala, belonged to a family that resided in Havana City, in close proximity to Infanta Street, during the late 1800s to early 1900s. Records indicate that Juan's siblings were married in El Cerro; however, comprehensive information regarding their baptismal records remains elusive. Ongoing research aims to establish a connection between this family and the Alcala families residing in Havana toward the end of the 18th century, tracing their roots back to Andalucia, Spain.

b027b2

 Q-M3

This lineage dates back ancestrally to the men who inhabited America prior to the European arrival. Currently it is difficult to find this type of haplogroups in many countries, being found in small isolated localities. In our case, it is the first paternal hapogroup with Native American origins that we found, and it will surely provide new data on the branches not yet discovered in America. (See our post)

Magdaleno Barcenas was a man who was born about 1830. He lived in the town of Sutiaba, a town known for their deep ties to their indigenous roots. He had at least 5 children with his wife Santos Sanchez. Current research is being carried out to learn the connection between the two Barcenas’ families. Descendants of Magdaleno and descendants of Eusebio.

dc6f04

 R-M269

Antonio Maceo, Major General, and Lieutenant of the Cuban Liberation Army, belonged to this line. A direct descendant of Maceo volunteered for this exam, and we learned about his European origins, going back to Valencia, Spain, in the 18th century.

Jose Muchuli and his son Miguel Muchuli Soler, both born in Valencia Spain 18th century. This last one traveled to Cuba where he had various children, one of which had a child with a Cuban parda whose last name was Macedo. There the paternal line takes the last name Maceo originating from Macedo.

59a75c

 R-M269

This line describes the genetic history of males with last name Almanza in Cuba. It is an antique last name, found in parish records of all regions, but in this case is referred to Central provinces of Cuba.

Juan Almanza was born in Sancti Spiritus province, probably in the decade of 1890s. He got married to Erundina Cruz and had offspring moving later to Havana and Matanzas.

0eaec7

 R-M269

This line describes the genetic history of males with last name Ricardo in Cuba. Such last name is very common in Holguin and its parent population Bayamo.

Antonio Emilio Ricardo Escalona was born probably around 1900 in Holguin, Holguin, Cuba. He got married to Aracelia Pupo and one of his great-grandson volunteered to do the DNA test with us.

02158e

 R-S933

This line collapses in one of the origins of Padron last name in Havana countryside area. Original from El Hierro, Islas Canarias, Sebastian Sanchez Padron went to Cuba and got married in Guanabacoa parish with Feliciana Muñoz de Cardenas. This couple had several descendants since the decade of 1730s, spreading through Peñalver, Santa Maria del Rosario, San Jose de las Lajas, Managua, Ciudad de la Habana, Güines, Tapaste and other surrounding areas.

Marcos Armas Arteaga, born c. 1560 in El Hierro, one of the Canary Islands. We know very little bout him, justthat he must passed awy before 1625 and that got married to Catalina Perez, having at leas one son called Juan Sanchez Arteaga.

a0db51

 A2m

An ancestral line of Cuban aborigines. The women of this maternal line initially lived in the central area of ​​Cuba, specifically in Quemado de Güines, Isabela de Sagua and Remedios, and later moved to the City of Havana. The haplogroup obtained is A2m, which is associated with the natives of Mexico, Cuba and Central America. Specialist Claudio M. Bravi analyzed the results and stated that this DNA presents 5 unique mutations that had not been seen before in scientific works, only coinciding with an individual from Missouri (according to the EMPOP database).

Maria Velis was a woman born c. 1770. Got married to Jose Milian and had a daughter in Guamutas (Marti), Matanzas Cuba.

d3897b

 A2m

Cuban native maternal DNA line. After a meticulous analysis of the results by Dr. Claudio M Bravi, it is concluded that this DNA has no matches seen so far, and the closest ones are with ancient Mexican samples.

Rosalia Olivera Sanz, born c. 1895 in current Granma province, Cuba.

af945b

 D1

Haplogroup of the mitochondrial DNA of a woman from Baracoa born in 1925. Given the distinctive features of the volunteer, and the family traditions, her genetics will provide knowledge about the haplogroups of Tainos in Eastern Cuba. Additional analysis (by Dr. Claudio M. Bravi) revealed an exact match of this mitochondrial sequence and that of a pre-Columbian sample from Curaçao.

 María Rita Lambert, born around the year 1870, who had children with Agustín Rodríguez (called an Indian from the eastern mountains, who hardly appeared publicly according to oral tradition). Further research is being developed by our volunteer in Guantanamo.

c8cf69

 A2q*

Maternal line of a probable new haplogroup. The closest prediction for this mt-DNA is A2q, but a closer look tells us that we might be in presence of a new classification due to unique mutations in specific loci. It is representative of Central American natives, specifically from Nicaragua.

Andrea Espinoza was a woman who was born in the mountains of northern Nicaragua. Where they are known for their beautiful woman and sugar mills. Andrea was born about 1870 and she had two daughters that we know of. Current research is being done to learn more about her maternal line and her haplogroup’s seemingly low presence in Nicaragua..

598e6b

 H3r

This maternal lineage belongs to a group of European women. The maternal genealogy of the volunteer develops in the fields of Eastern Havana. It has an exact DNA match to a maternal line that lived near the Cienfuegos area of ​​Cuba, but the common ancestor has not yet been found in genealogy.

Rosa Gomez was a woman born c. 1860 in the Habana del Este area. She got married to Manuel Hernandez and lived between Jaruco and Campo Florido.

4aaff6

 K1a4a1

Maternal line with European ancient origins that lived in Havana 19th century.

Mariana Ramos, born c. 1826 in Centro Habana, la Habana Cuba. She was married to Jose de la Luz Cairo and their daughter was baptized and married in Nuestra Señora del Monserrate parish, in Centro Habana.

8d19f8

 L1c1a

This was our first maternal african line examined. Represents a haplogrupo present in some pygmy tribes of West Africa, specially in the congolese area. (See our post)

Joaquina Calvo was an African woman taken to Cuba in the second half of the 19th century. I work at the Ingenio Portugalete sugar mill, in San Jose de las Lajas, owned by Manuel Calvo, for which he was given that last name. He had a daughter in 1880 where it is stated that Jacoba was of the Conga nation, and a free person, a worker at said sugar mill. We do not know data about his life at a more advanced age.
